Personal Prevention

Simple actions that meaningfully reduce tick bites.

  • Perform thorough tick checks after time outdoors — inspect arms, legs, scalp, behind knees, groin, and behind ears.
  • Wear protective clothing: long sleeves, long pants tucked into socks, light colors to spot ticks quickly.
  • Use EPA‑registered repellents containing DEET, picaridin, IR3535, or oil of lemon eucalyptus on skin. Follow label instructions.
  • Use permethrin‑treated clothing or gear for extended protection. Treat footwear, pants, and outer layers per product directions.
  • Shower within two hours of being outdoors to wash off unattached ticks and make checks easier.
  • Know the peak seasons locally (spring–early summer for many species), and increase vigilance then.

What to do if you find a tick

  • Remove promptly with fine‑tipped tweezers, grasping the tick close to the skin and pulling straight out. Clean the bite site with soap and water or alcohol.
  • Save the tick in a sealed container if you want it tested or identified later.
  • Watch for symptoms for 30 days: fever, rash, joint pain, or flu‑like illness — seek medical care if symptoms occur.

Gear & Testing

  • Tick removal tool / fine tweezers: inexpensive, reusable, and faster than fingernails.
  • Repellents: DEET 20–30% or picaridin 20% are commonly recommended for long protection.
  • Permethrin clothing: factory‑treated or home‑treated with permethrin for durable protection (follow safety guidelines).
  • Testing: Not all labs will test ticks for pathogens. If you saved the tick, check local public health or university extension services for testing availability.

Landscape & Community Measures

Actions that reduce tick habitat around homes and communities.

  • Maintain short grass and remove leaf litter and brush from yard edges.
  • Create a 3‑ to 9‑foot woodchip or gravel barrier between forested areas and lawns or play areas.
  • Discourage deer and rodents near homes (secure trash, bird seed, and compost bins).
  • For larger programs, targeted acaricide application and host‑targeted treatments can reduce tick abundance — consult local public health or extension services.
