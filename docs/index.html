Injury Prevention

Simple actions that can help prevent injury.

  • Strength training
  • Stretching/Yoga
  • Endurance training
  • Stabilizing footwear
  • Using hiking poles/sticks

Factors that increase the risk of injury

  • No prior hiking experience
  • Greater pack weight to body weight ratio
  • BMI greater than 30
  • Previous injuries that required a doctor's visit

Hydration

Water alone may not be enough to keep you hydrated on a hot sunny day. Opt for drinks containing a balance of water, electrolytes, and carbohydrates to keep you optimally hydrated. Some examples include:.

  • Hydralyte Sports
  • Mizone
  • Powerade Zero
  • DIY Mix: 250 mL of juice + 750mL water + a pinch of salt

Rabies

Prevention and Education

  • Greater than 90% of reported rabid animals in the U.S. are wildlife. There are no current secondary intervention methods for the prevention of rabies. Therefore, education is the key to prevention.

    • Transmission

  • Spread through saliva and/or a bite from a rabid animal.
  • AVOID handling all dead and alive wild animals: If a dead animal, suspected of having rabies, is found on a trail, note location and report to local wilderness authorities for management and disposal.

    • When to seek care

  • Exposure requires a hospital visit ASAP to receive post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP)!
  • Symptoms of rabies in humans range from: behavioral changes to non-specific symptoms such as fever, headaches, nausea, and vomiting.

Ticks

Ticks are a potential vector for diseases such as Lyme disease and Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever. These illnesses can lead to potentially fatal side effects if not treated early or prevented all together.

  • Use of a insect repellent that includes DEET or Picaridin before embarking on the hike can greatly reduce the risk of getting bitten by a tick. Avoiding walking through thick vegetation can also decrease your chances of encountering ticks.
  • It is not uncommon to find a tick embedded in the skin after spending an afternoon in the mountains. Common places to find ticks on the body include warm moist areas such as the scalp, armpits, groin, behind the knees and along the waistline.
  • The most effective method to remove an embedded tick is to use tweezers to gently squeeze the head of the tick and pull it up. Maintain consistent pressure until the tick releases.
